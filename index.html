AN INVESTIGATION OF SEASONAL VARIATION OF POLYCYCLIC AROMATIC HYDROCARBON WITH GC-FID AND THEIR SOURCES APPORTIONMENT STUDY WITH PMF MODEL IN DHAKA, BANGLADESH

Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs) are a large group of organic compounds that have two or more aromatic (benzene) rings fused together. The PAHs emissions were large in the winter than in the summer, with a difference of about 1.3 folds between the month with the largest and smallest emissions. Three possible mechanisms of PAH formation during combustion, i.e. slow Diels–Alder condensations, rapid radical reactions, and ionic reaction mechanism. However, the radical formation mechanism is favored. The major sources of PAH emissions include domestic and industrial sources, mobile emissions, industrial, and agricultural activities, and natural sources. Different types of PAHs in the air have different short-term and long-term effects on human health. PAHs can affect eye and nose irritation; inflammation; DNA, cataracts, kidney, and liver damage ever can cause gastrovascular cancer. The objective of the study is to determine the concentration of PAHs in monsoon and winter seasons, determine their sources, and Potential Risk Assessment on human health.

The exact sampling location is situated on the roof of MHS Building, Department of Chemistry, University of Dhaka (DU). The wind direction in Dhaka city is mainly from the west and south-west direction in pre-monsoon, and from north and north-west in winter. Aerosol particulate matter was collected with a High volume sampler (Model ZARGES 40566) on Quartz filters (Gelman, Membrane Filters, Type TISSU Quartz 2500QAT-UP, 15cm diameter). The loaded Quartz filters were kept in the refrigerator at 40C until chemical analysis to limit the losses of the components. The particulate PAHs-containing sample was weighed and a solvent solution of about 15 mL acetone was added to dissolve the PAHs. The sample solution was kept for 24 hours then sonication was done. After sonication, the extract was filtered through Whatman 41 filter paper and collected in a clean volumetric flask. Now the solution is ready for analysis. The PAHs were analyzed using Gas Chromatography (GC) and Flame Photometric Detector (FPD). GC is used in analytical chemistry for separating and analyzing compounds in the vaporized state without thermal decomposition. The reason to use more than one kind of detector for GC is to achieve selective and/or highly sensitive detection of specific compounds encountered in particular chromatographic analyses. The determination of sulfur or phosphorus-containing compounds is the job of the FPD. Within an ECD, when certain molecules pass by the detector, they capture some of the electrons in the sample and this reduces the current measured.

The higher PAHs emission occurring in the winter-time can cause higher exposure of PAHs to humans and make larger outflow flux in the winter. The concentration of Cymoxanil was relatively higher than that of Naphthalene, Anthracene, Butachlor and Diazinon in monsoon season, and the concentration of Cymoxanil and Naphthalene was relatively higher than that of Anthracene, Butachlor, and Diazinon in the winter season. The main source of Naphthalene was Gasoline Exhaust (52.3% and 50.7%) found in both seasons, Anthracene was Diesel Exhaust (71.0%) in winter, Cymoxanil was Diesel Exhaust (63.0% and 39.0%) and Diazinon was Industries (74.8% and 50.1%) in both seasons were determined by PMF method. Potential health risk assessment was calculated with conventional approaches (HQ and LCR, US EPA). The result showed that a lifetime cancer risk (LCR) for Naphthalene and Anthracene in the air was an acceptable value. In reference to the hazard quotient (HQ), the calculated value was less than 1 for Naphthalene. It indicated that Naphthalene has a potential noncarcinogenic impact on human health.

Optical Properties of Brown Carbon (BrC) Emission from Biomasses and Fossil Fuels Combustion: Impacts of pH and Solvents

Brown carbon aerosols are non-graphitic organic materials that are brown or yellowish in color. Brown carbon aerosols are made of thousands of different complex compounds. This class of organic carbon absorbs strongly in the ultraviolet wavelengths. Measuring BrC absorption is also essential due to its effect on photolysis rates and solar UV radiation reaching the surface.

Cooking stoves, gas meter, suction pump, sample holder, movable table, normal table, and plastic pot comprises the total arrangement for sampling the fourteen different biomasses and fossil fuel samples that were collected in Quartz filters (Gelman, membrane filters, type tissue quartz 2500 qat-up, 47 mm diameter). Before sampling each quartz filter paper was heated at 800° C for 4 hours to remove all organic impurities. The sample holder filled with quartz filters was placed about 31 cm on top of the cooking stove. About 50.0 g of each biomass was fed into the cooking stove for the combustion experiment. Deposited PM weight was calculated from the differences between PM loaded and unloaded filters. The volume of air was recorded from the difference between the initial and final gas meter readings. The PM-loaded filters were kept in a Petri-dish and preserved in a desiccator to prevent moister at control temperature and humidity. For measurements of both WSOC and MeSOC; a quarter portion of each filter sample (3.46 cm2) was pre-soaked overnight and sonicated for 60 min with 25 mL (total volume) of Milli-Q water and methanol (MeOH). Then, each extract was filtered through a filter in an amber glass vial. To analyze the effect of pH, the pH of WSOC extracted solutions were adjusted to 2.0, 6.0 and 10.0 using dilute HCl or NaOH. Then The light absorption characteristics of the samples were determined by UV- Vis spectrophotometer.

The effects of pH and solvent on the light absorption properties of Brown Carbon (BrC) from biomass and fossil fuel combustion and total organic carbon (TOC) concentration were investigated. The light absorption properties of water-soluble BrC are highly dependent on pH. BrC exhibited greater light absorption in water that than in methanol. babs-365, MAE365, AAE and kabs-365 of BrC in water and methanol extracts were 79806.9 and 93626 Mm−1, 2.51 and 3.34 m2 g−1, 1.97 and 2.66 (200–700 nm), 0.07 and 0.9, respectively. Kerosene have the highest and jute stick has the lowest babs in water and methanol (257154.17 and 288184.72 Mm−1; 4726.66 and 3338.21 Mm−1, respectively). MAE365 in water has highest value in diesel (6.69 m2 g−1) and in methanol, highest value is in jackfruit tree (13.02 m2 g−1). In both solvent jute stick has lowest MAE365 (0.1 m2 g−1 and 0.14 m2 g−1). In both water and methanol, cow dung has highest AAE (4.34 and 6.07), whereas petrol has lowest (0.33- 0.41). Comparison of babs365 between methanol and water extracts suggested that a large portion of BrC light absorption comes from water insoluble organic carbon. Significantly lower value of AAE highlights more light absorbing potential of atmospheric BrC. Variation of kabs of BrC is found for different biomasses whereas similarity is observed for fossil fuels. The average concentration of TOC in biomasses and fossil fuels are 27.54 and 22.83 ppm, respectively. Cow dung has the highest TOC (83.02 ppm) and koroi has the lowest (17.71 ppm).
